Is COVID-19 Affecting Census Response Rates?¶

By Benjamin Livingston, NewsCounts¶

March 25, 2020¶

Introduction¶

COVID-19 has shut down much of America, including the United States Census Bureau's field operations.

This raises the question - it there a correlation between COVID-19 prevalence and census response rates?

We aim to answer that question with a brief exploration here.

To do this, we examined county-level COVID-19 data that was readily available, and paired it with the official census return rate numbers to gauge a possible connection. We then generated infection rates for each county by normalizing on each county's population estimate from the 2018 American Community Survey.

There are a few important caveats here:

  • COVID-19 data is believed to be highly inaccurate, and may be better in areas with certain demographics (skewing this data), perhaps due to more widespread testing and case tracing
  • On a similar note, many cases in some states have not been assigned to a county, and have a county label as "unknown"; thus, while examining data at the county level gives us the power of granularity, there is some information loss
  • The vast majority of counties have zero reported cases - thus, we will consider two datasets, one including those zero values, and one not including those zero values.

Our findings are presented below.

Results¶

Effects On Various Socioeconomic Groups¶

The first thing to examine is this: is COVID-19 affecting various hard-to-count groups differently?

I will use a log scale for COVID-19 cases, in order to properly reflect that they are growing exponentially. In short, this will allow us to better gauge the relationship between prevalence of reported COVID-19 cases and prevalence of certain demographic groups.

I'll also normalize infection totals by county population, so we get infection rates, rather than raw numbers of infections.

For reference while reading these results: correlations range on a constant scale from -1 (perfectly negatively correlated), to 0 (absolutely no correlation), to 1 (perfectly positively correlated) - which is to say if a value is very close to zero, two factors are not correlated. Generally, a correlation would need to be at or above .3 or at or below -.3 for us to consider it at least moderately significant.

It is vital to note that correlation does not imply causation - we are simply noting that areas with certain demographics tend to have higher (or lower) reported infection rates (and again, these are reported, county-assigned infections), not definitely stating that certain groups are infected by the virus more frequently than others.

Also, we're using classic linear correlations (Pearson correlations) with an exponential transformation on the number of cases, which is only one kind of correlation - so just because we don't find a relationship here doesn't mean necessarily mean one doesn't exist.

All Counties¶

Factoring in all counties - not just those with reported cases - we see a few interesting trends.

More populous counties tend to have lower reported infection rates - there's a moderate correlation there. This could be because:

  • More populous areas are less prone to infection
  • More populous areas have more difficulty robustly testing their large population
  • Cases are fairly evenly spread throughout the country regardless of population density
  • Any of a number of other factors

...but we can't say for sure.

We don't see too much correlation between prevalence of certain racial groups and rate of reported COVID-19 infections, except for American Indians & Alaska Natives (AIAN) - perhaps their natural social distancing and distance from population centers could be an asset here.

While the correlation is weak, it is interesting to note that areas with more minors tend to have have slightly lower infection rates, which is unsurprising given that COVID-19 is well-known to have a greater effect on older adults, which may lead to adult cases being reported more frequently.

It is also worth noting that we observe the correlation between prevalence of foreign-born populations and prevalence of reported COVID-19 infections is positive, but very slight.

Finally, we note the negative correlation between poverty and prevalence of positive COVID-19 tests - this number isn't particularly significant, but this would stand in line with the belief that underprivleged citizens are not receiving proper testing. So perhaps it would not make sense to assume that poorer populations are being infected less frequently.

It seems that, generally, outside of the AIAN population, racial and economic demographics seem to correlate little with COVID-19 infection rates.

Effect On Census Response Rates¶

We will now examine the correlation between census response rates and prevalence of positive COVID-19 cases

For reference while reading these results:

  • Internet rates are calculated as the percent of all housing units who responded online.
  • Overall response rates are calculated as the percent of online, mail, and phone self-responses collectively.
  • Correlations range on a constant scale from -1 (perfectly negatively correlated), to 0 (absolutely no correlation), to 1 (perfectly positively correlated) - which is to say if a value is very close to zero, two factors are not correlated.

All Counties¶

Factoring in all counties - not just those with reported cases - we see a fairly substantial negative correlation between COVID-19 cases and census self-response rates.

This means that where there are more COVID-19 cases, people tend to fill out their census forms less, to a pretty noticeable degree.

Looking For Confounding Variables¶

We know that census response rates tend to vary in areas with different demographics.

With that in mind, I looked across all counties in our dataset to see if prevalence of COVID-19 still negatively correlated with the "overall" census response rates after conditioning on several demographic factors.

In other words, is this a real relationship, and not a mirage simply caused by the demographics of certain areas?

Generally, if the latter was true, these two variables would have a partial correlation close to zero (as pretty much all the demographic factors do) - and as you can see below, that wasn't the case, so these demographic factors do not appear to be behind the negative correlation between COVID-19 prevalence and census response rates.

Conclusion¶

Overall, we find that counties with higher reported infection rates have generally filled out census forms at a slower rate.

It is very important to note that confounding variables may play a role here, so we can't claim that COVID-19 is slowing census responses. It may be that other demographic characteristics that we did not examine here lead to higher COVID-19 infection rates also lead to lower census response rates.

As an example of how these numbers can be misleading, note that the raw number of COVID-19 cases is actually positively correlated with census return rates, possibly because urban areas typically have higher return rates than rural areas. We considered county population in this model, but perhaps there are other confounding variables we have overlooked.

That being said, we do observe that higher reported COVID-19 infection rates have tended to pair with somewhat lower census response rates, even when factoring out basic socioeconomic characteristics of counties.
